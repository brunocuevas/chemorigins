ChemOrigins

Wellcome to ChemOrigins, a community-based effort to build a database for Prebiotic Chemistry. Specifically, we aim to compile experimentally tested prebiotic pathways to produce biomolecules or other molecules relevant for the Origin of Life.

FAQ

What is prebiotic chemistry?

Although there are endless debates about the Origin of Life, most scientists would agree that life emerged from chemistry. The question is "which chemistry". Most prebiotic chemists try to explain how complex biomolecules would emerge the chemistry that was feasible in the ancient-Earth where life originated around 3.8 Gya.


Prebiotic chemistry is a unique field that requires understanding biology, chemistry and geology simulatenously. Although the goal is extraordinarily challenging, prebiotic chemistry has reached many different milestones, such as the synthesis of nucleosides or aminoacids.

Why ChemOrigins?

After the first succesful prebiotic chemistry experiments, in 1953, prebiotic chemists have generated a great amount of knowledge about the prebiotically viable conidtions that would allow the formation of biomolecules in the deep-past. However, navigating this knowledge is far from trivial, usually requiring endless hours of literature digest.


Scientists in other adjacent fields have benefited from having comprehensive databases allowing scientists and machines to access the data free of cost. Therefore, we aspire to become an important source to retrieve information about prebiotic chemistry.

What can I find here?

ChemOrigins provides access to a knowledge graph of prebiotic chemistry, where molecules have been connected with the reactions that produce them or consume them, and the reactions with the evidence where they were detected. This is possible through the literature curation of experimental prebiotic chemistry and the analysis of such a knowledge using a graph database.


Questions such as "how can be alanine abiotically produced?", "under which temperatures has the synthesis of nucleotides measured?" or "which reactions are catalyzed by magnetite" are the kind of questions that we aim to address with this database.

How can I contribute?

You can contribute by joining the discussion in the Github repository. You can either start new threads in the issues, or fork the repository to annotate new reactions.

Who is building the database?

The database was built and is currently mantained by the Kaçar lab at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, and by Bruno Cuevas-Zuviría at the Universidad Politécnica de Madrid
